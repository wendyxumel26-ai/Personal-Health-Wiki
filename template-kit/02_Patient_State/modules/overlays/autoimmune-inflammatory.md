## ⚠️ Analytical Defaults (this module)

1. **A positive ANA is not a diagnosis** — ANA positivity is common in the general population; the titre and pattern determine clinical significance; ANA ≥1:160 with an appropriate clinical picture warrants specialist review; an isolated transient positive requires monitoring, not a diagnostic label
2. **ANA titre can fluctuate** — a high titre during illness or inflammation may not persist; serial monitoring matters more than any single result; document the trajectory
3. **ANA pattern informs differential** — speckled pattern (anti-ENA antibodies) has a different differential from homogeneous (anti-dsDNA), centromere, or nucleolar patterns; note pattern alongside titre
4. **Ferritin as an inflammatory marker** — ferritin is a positive acute phase reactant; a high ferritin during active inflammation or illness does not reflect iron stores; interpret only in confirmed stable, non-inflammatory state
5. **CRP and ESR measure different things** — CRP rises within hours of acute inflammation and falls quickly; ESR rises more slowly and reflects chronic or more sustained inflammation; both normal does not exclude autoimmune disease
6. **Organ-specific antibodies must be tested to rule out organ-specific autoimmune disease** — anti-LKM (autoimmune hepatitis), AMA (primary biliary cholangitis), anti-TPO (thyroid), anti-GBM (kidney) are examples; ANA alone cannot exclude these
7. **Rheumatological workup is a process, not a single test** — initial ANA positivity should trigger further testing for specific antibodies, complement levels, and clinical review; absence of a specific antibody does not always exclude the associated condition
8. **Stress and immune function are linked** — immune dysregulation, transient ANA positivity, and inflammatory marker elevation have been documented in association with sustained psychological stress; this context should be part of any autoimmune assessment

## 6. Cross-System Links

**Autoimmune → Liver:** Autoimmune hepatitis and primary biliary cholangitis both present with liver inflammation and abnormal LFTs. These should be excluded when liver inflammation is present — anti-LKM and AMA are the key tests. See `metabolic-liver.md`.

**Autoimmune → Haematology:** Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, immune-mediated thrombocytopenia, and leucopenia can occur in systemic autoimmune conditions (e.g. SLE). Haematological findings should be interpreted in the context of any autoimmune workup. See `haematology.md`.

**Autoimmune → Thyroid:** Thyroid autoimmunity (Hashimoto's, Graves') is common in patients with other autoimmune conditions and vice versa. Anti-TPO should be part of any autoimmune screen when thyroid function fluctuates.

**Stress → Immune:** Sustained psychological stress can affect immune function and inflammatory markers. If you observe a temporal pattern between high-stress periods and immune dysregulation (elevated inflammatory markers, ANA fluctuation, symptom flares), document it explicitly — it may be clinically significant and is worth raising with your clinician.

**ANA → Pharmacological caution:** Some medications can trigger drug-induced lupus (e.g. hydralazine, isoniazid, procainamide, minocycline). Note any medication-related ANA triggers in the history.
