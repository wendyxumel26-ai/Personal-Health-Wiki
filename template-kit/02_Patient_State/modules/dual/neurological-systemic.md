# Neurological Module — Systemic Symptoms
> **Scope:** Tracking neurological symptoms that arise as a consequence of, or alongside, another primary chronic condition — rather than a primary neurological diagnosis.
> **Category:** Dual-mode — use this file when neurological symptoms are secondary. Use `neurological-primary.md` instead if a neurological condition is your primary diagnosis.
> **When to use this file:** You have noticed symptoms such as tingling, numbness, brain fog, balance problems, poor sleep, or restless legs — and these appear in the context of a primary condition such as diabetes, liver disease, autoimmune conditions, kidney disease, or IBD.
> **Always read alongside `index.md`** — this module does not repeat demographic, lifestyle, or cross-system context.
> **Last updated:** [DATE]

## ⚠️ Analytical Defaults (this module)

1. **Systemic neurological symptoms are frequently underdiagnosed** — patients do not typically connect tingling feet, brain fog, or restless legs to a neurological process; the framework must make this link explicit
2. **The mechanism matters as much as the symptom** — peripheral neuropathy, autonomic dysfunction, and cognitive impairment each arise via different pathways and point to different primary causes; identify the mechanism, not just the experience
3. **Neurological symptoms may precede abnormal blood markers** — B12 deficiency causes neurological damage before anaemia appears; peripheral neuropathy in diabetes can precede formal diagnosis; do not use a normal blood result to dismiss a symptom
4. **Multiple contributing causes are common** — B12 deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, and anaemia can all produce overlapping neurological symptoms simultaneously; assess each independently
5. **Symptom trajectory is as important as symptom presence** — new, worsening, or changing neurological symptoms in the context of a chronic condition warrant active investigation; stable residual symptoms warrant monitoring
6. **Autonomic symptoms are often missed** — dizziness on standing, temperature dysregulation, bladder changes, and sweating abnormalities are neurological symptoms that patients rarely identify as such

## 5. Cross-System Links

**Neurological ← Haematology:** B12 deficiency causes neurological damage before haematological changes appear. Anaemia reduces oxygen delivery and produces fatigue and cognitive symptoms. Iron deficiency (even without anaemia) affects energy and concentration. See `core/haematology.md`.

**Neurological ← Liver:** Minimal hepatic encephalopathy — subtle cognitive slowing and attention deficits — is recognised in liver disease earlier than previously assumed. Liver disease also impairs B12/folate processing, compounding nutritional neurological risk. See `conditions/metabolic-liver.md`.

**Neurological ← Thyroid:** Both hypothyroidism (cognitive slowing, fatigue, peripheral neuropathy) and hyperthyroidism (anxiety, tremor, palpitations) produce neurological symptoms. TSH trend is a standard part of any systemic neurological symptom workup. See `conditions/hormonal-reproductive.md`.

**Neurological ← Autoimmune:** Small fibre neuropathy is increasingly recognised across autoimmune conditions, often before a formal diagnosis is established. If autoimmune markers are active, consider neurological involvement. See `overlays/autoimmune-inflammatory.md`.

**Neurological ← Stress:** Chronic stress affects sleep quality, pain thresholds, HPA axis function, and cognitive performance. Fatigue and cognitive symptoms attributed to stress may have additional physiological contributors — always assess the blood markers in Section 4 before attributing symptoms to stress alone.

**If symptoms suggest a primary neurological condition:** See `dual/neurological-primary.md` — the fuller diagnostic and treatment tracking module.
