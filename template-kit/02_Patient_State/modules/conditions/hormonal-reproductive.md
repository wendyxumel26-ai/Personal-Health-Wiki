## ⚠️ Analytical Defaults (this module)

1. **Clinical staging and biochemical staging are different** — perimenopause can be present without abnormal FSH/LH; symptoms and cycle changes are diagnostic evidence; biochemical confirmation is a separate (and sometimes lagging) data point
2. **TSH alone is insufficient for thyroid assessment** — if TSH is abnormal or borderline, add Free T4 and Free T3 to characterise direction and severity; subclinical hypo/hyperthyroidism has systemic implications beyond thyroid itself
3. **Thyroid function is not static** — TSH fluctuates, particularly during perimenopause; a single normal reading does not confirm stable thyroid; document the trend across multiple readings
4. **Thyroid history must be disclosed before starting hormonal or GLP-1 treatments** — thyroid contraindications exist for several medications commonly used in this space
5. **Reproductive conditions drive systemic haematological effects** — adenomyosis and fibroids cause menstrual blood loss → iron deficiency → anaemia; this loop cannot be resolved by iron management alone without addressing the gynaecological cause
6. **Hormonal panels must be timed correctly** — FSH and oestradiol are most informative on day 2–5 of the menstrual cycle; AMH can be done at any time; results outside this window are harder to interpret
7. **Adenomyosis symptoms typically worsen through perimenopause** — do not assume stability; trajectory should be expected to change as hormonal milieu shifts
8. **Biotin supplementation interferes with thyroid immunoassays** — if biotin is being taken, flag to lab and clinician; results may be unreliable

## 6. Cross-System Links

**Gynaecology → Haematology:** Menstrual blood loss from adenomyosis/fibroids is a direct driver of iron deficiency and low-normal Hb. The haematological picture cannot improve without addressing the gynaecological cause. See `haematology.md`.

**Thyroid → Lipids:** Hypothyroidism raises triglycerides; hyperthyroidism lowers them. Document direction of any thyroid fluctuation when interpreting lipid trends.

**Thyroid → MCV:** Hypothyroidism is a recognised cause of macrocytosis. If MCV is above personal baseline, assess thyroid as a contributing cause before attributing to liver disease or nutritional deficiency.

**Perimenopause → Lipids:** Oestrogen decline in perimenopause is associated with rising LDL and triglycerides and falling HDL. Note hormonal context when interpreting lipid trajectory in perimenopausal patients.

**Perimenopause → Bone:** If not already tracked, note that oestrogen decline accelerates bone loss. Baseline DEXA may be warranted depending on other risk factors.

**Hormonal treatment → Thyroid monitoring:** Starting any hormonal treatment (HRT, contraception) can affect thyroid function, particularly in patients with pre-existing thyroid fluctuation. Test TSH before and after starting.
