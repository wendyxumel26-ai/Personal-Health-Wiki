# Appointment Preparation Template
> **Scope:** Australian healthcare system. Covers GP and specialist appointments.
> **Usage:** Copy and fill in for each upcoming appointment. File completed versions in your wiki as a record of what was discussed and decided.
> **Read alongside:** `index.md` (for open questions and pending items) and the relevant module file for the system being discussed.

## Australian Appointment Reality Check

Before preparing, know what you are working with:

**Standard GP consultation (Item 23):** Up to 20 minutes on paper; in practice often 10–12 minutes of actual consultation time after admin. Fits 1–2 focused topics at most.

**Long GP consultation (Item 36):** 20–40 minutes. Request explicitly when booking. Carries a gap payment at most non-bulk-billing practices. Use this for: first appointments with a new GP, results reviews involving multiple systems, treatment decisions, or care plan reviews.

**Prolonged GP consultation (Item 44):** 40+ minutes. Rarely offered without specific clinical justification, but can be requested. Use for: comprehensive case reviews, preparing a Chronic Disease Management (CDM) plan, or major treatment decision appointments.

**Specialist appointment:** First appointments are typically 30–45 minutes in private practice, less in public outpatient clinics. Review appointments are often 15–20 minutes. Public hospital outpatient clinics run to strict time — arrive with your priorities ranked.

**The core constraint:** In a standard GP appointment you can realistically address one clinical topic with any depth, or two light topics. If you have more than that, either book a long consultation or prioritise ruthlessly and save the rest for next time.

## Part 2 — During the Appointment

### Pacing for a standard consultation (~10 minutes)

| Time | What to do |
|------|-----------|
| Minutes 1–2 | State your primary goal clearly and briefly. Hand over SBAR if new clinician. |
| Minutes 3–8 | The clinical conversation. Stay on the primary goal. Resist the pull to add more. |
| Minutes 8–9 | Confirm what's been decided / ordered / referred. Get specific. |
| Minute 10 | Ask: *"Is there anything you need from me before the next appointment?"* |

**If they start running short:** *"I have one more thing I really need to cover — can we take another two minutes, or should I book a follow-up for that?"* This is better than rushing and leaving unclear.

### Pacing for a long consultation (20–40 minutes)

| Time | What to do |
|------|-----------|
| Minutes 1–3 | Context-setting. SBAR overview if new or changed. State all three goals. |
| Minutes 4–20 | Primary goal in depth. Let the conversation develop. |
| Minutes 20–30 | Secondary goal. |
| Minutes 30–35 | Confirm decisions, tests, referrals. Summarise aloud what has been agreed. |
| Minutes 35–40 | Next steps — who does what by when. Book follow-up if needed. |

### During the conversation — things to listen for

- **Any new diagnosis or label** — ask how confident they are and what would change their assessment
- **Any test ordered** — confirm: what is it testing for, when will results be available, and how will you receive them (do they call, or do you need to follow up?)
- **Any referral written** — ask: who specifically are they referring you to, is it public or private, what is the likely wait time, and can you see a copy of the referral letter?
- **Any medication change** — confirm: what is it for, what are the monitoring requirements, and does it interact with anything on your current list?

### The coordination gap reminder

If you see a specialist and are returning to your GP (or vice versa), bring any specialist letters you have received. Do not assume the letter has arrived — in Australia, specialist letters to GPs can take 2–4 weeks and occasionally are not sent at all. You are the most reliable information carrier in your own care network.

## Australian-Specific Notes

### MyHealth Record
If you are enrolled in MyHealth Record (myhealth.gov.au), some test results, specialist letters, and clinical notes may be uploaded by your providers. Check it after each appointment — not all providers upload reliably, but it is worth reviewing. You can also upload documents yourself and control which providers can access your record.

### Requesting copies of your results
You are legally entitled to your own health information in Australia. You can request:
- Copies of blood test results (directly from the pathology lab, or via your GP)
- Copies of imaging reports and images (from the radiology provider)
- Copies of specialist letters your GP has received (ask your GP to print or forward them)
- A copy of any referral letter your GP writes for you (ask at the time it is written)

Getting into the habit of requesting these proactively closes the information gap that affects most complex patients.

### Chronic Disease Management (CDM) Plans
If you have two or more chronic conditions being managed by your GP, you may be eligible for a CDM plan (Medicare Item 721) and Team Care Arrangement (Item 723). These entitle you to:
- A formal GP-prepared care plan covering your conditions, goals, and treatment
- Up to 5 Medicare-rebated allied health sessions per year (dietitian, exercise physiologist, psychologist, physiotherapist, etc.)
- Annual review appointments (Item 732)

If you have not had a CDM plan discussed and you have multiple active chronic conditions, ask your GP explicitly at your next long consultation. It is often not raised proactively.

### Gap payments and out-of-pocket costs
Not all GP services are bulk-billed. Long consultations at non-bulk-billing practices carry gap payments — typically $20–$60 above the Medicare rebate, depending on the practice. This is a real cost but is appropriate for the level of care complex patients need. Budget for it in your ongoing health management.
