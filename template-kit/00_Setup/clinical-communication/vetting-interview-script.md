# Vetting Interview Script — The First Consultation
> **Scope:** Australian healthcare system.
> **Purpose:** Test whether a candidate GP is suited to complex chronic illness management before revealing the full depth of your case. One appointment to assess mindset — not to solve problems.
> **Prerequisites:** Complete `doctor-sourcing-guide.md` first. This script is for your shortlisted 2–3 candidates.

---

## The Core Principle: The Tasting Menu

Do not arrive at a first consultation with a complex, multi-system chronic patient and lay out everything. You will overwhelm a standard GP and make them defensive. You will also lose your ability to observe their natural response to uncertainty — which is exactly what you are there to measure.

The first appointment is a **tasting menu**, not a full meal. You are there to assess the GP's mindset with a carefully calibrated amount of information. If the fit is confirmed, you can deepen the relationship over subsequent appointments and eventually bring the full picture.

The goal: leave knowing whether this GP is a collaborative partner or not. That is the only decision this appointment needs to make.

---

## Step 1 — Book the Right Appointment

In Australia, GP consultations are billed under Medicare item numbers by length:
- **Level B (Item 23):** Up to 20 minutes — the standard consultation
- **Level C (Item 36):** 20–40 minutes — "long consultation"
- **Level D (Item 44):** 40+ minutes — "prolonged consultation"

**Book a long consultation (Level C) for this first appointment.** Say explicitly when booking: *"I'd like to book a long consultation — I have a complex health situation I want to discuss with the GP for the first time."* Most practices that offer this option will accommodate it. If the practice only books standard 10-minute slots with no option for longer, that is itself a signal (see `doctor-sourcing-guide.md` red flags).

Note: a long consultation will carry a gap payment at most non-bulk-billing practices. This is expected and appropriate — the extra investment is in assessing whether this GP is worth the ongoing relationship.

---

## Step 2 — What to Bring

Bring a **one-page summary only** — not the full wiki. Use the SBAR Briefing (`sbar-briefing-template.md`) as your one-page document. It contains:
- Your active conditions (in plain language)
- Your current care team
- 2–3 open questions you genuinely want help with

Do not bring the full index.md, your module files, or a stack of blood test printouts. You are not asking them to read your research — you are observing how they respond to a well-organised patient with a complex presentation.

---

## Step 3 — The Opening

Keep the opening factual and brief. Do not start with a history that runs 10 minutes — you need time to observe their response.

Suggested opening (adapt to your own words):

> *"I've got a complex health situation that spans a few systems — liver, blood, and gynaecology at the moment. I've been tracking it carefully with another GP who has been coordinating things, and I'm looking for a longer-term GP partner who is comfortable with this kind of ongoing, multi-system case. I've brought a one-page summary. Would it be useful if I walked you through it?"*

Hand them the SBAR briefing. Give them 60–90 seconds to read it silently. Watch what they do with it.

---

## Step 4 — The Test Question

After they have reviewed the summary, ask the central vetting question. Deliver it directly, without apology:

> *"I have a complex presentation that hasn't fit standard pathways in some areas. I'm looking for a GP who is comfortable running thorough exclusionary testing and collaborating with specialists over the long term — treating the full picture rather than each system separately. Is that a style of practice you enjoy, or is your schedule currently too demanding for that kind of depth?"*

The second half of the question is intentional. It gives them an honest exit — *"my schedule is very full right now"* — which is more useful information than a reflexive *"yes of course."* A standard GP who knows they are not suited will sometimes take the exit. A Coordinating GP will not need it.

Then stop talking. Let them respond fully before adding anything.

---

## Step 5 — The Reaction Matrix

Observe their response across four bands:

### Band A — Confirmed Coordinating GP
They engage directly with the complexity. They may ask a clarifying question about one of the systems on the summary. They comment on a connection between findings. They say something like *"this is exactly the kind of case I find interesting"* or *"I'd want to understand the transferrin pattern better."* They do not reach for a simple explanation.

**What to do:** Ask the follow-up question (Step 6). This is your GP.

### Band B — Probable Coordinating GP, needs confirmation
They respond positively but stay general — *"yes, I'm comfortable with complex cases"* without engaging with anything specific in your summary. They may seem interested but not yet show the depth of curiosity you need. They do not deflect, but they do not light up either.

**What to do:** Ask the follow-up question (Step 6) and weigh the whole appointment before deciding.

### Band C — Structurally limited standard GP
They are warm and willing but immediately cite time constraints: *"I'd love to help but I'm only doing 10-minute appointments at the moment"* or *"we'd need to break this into several visits."* They are not wrong — they are being honest about a structural problem. The practice model does not support what you need.

**What to do:** Thank them, complete the appointment, do not return. The limitation is the practice, not necessarily the GP — but the outcome is the same.

### Band D — Dispositional standard GP
They show mild irritation at the complexity, immediately simplify (*"let's just focus on the liver for today"*), suggest you may be over-investigating, or give you the sense that your level of engagement with your own health is a problem. They may be dismissive of specialist-level thinking from a patient.

**What to do:** Thank them, complete the appointment, do not return. Note this as a strong signal and remove from your shortlist.

---

## Step 6 — The Follow-Up Question

If the GP is Band A or B, ask one more question to assess their approach to uncertainty and specialist coordination:

> *"One of the challenges I've had is that the different specialists involved don't communicate with each other — I'm essentially the person carrying information between them. Is coordinating across multiple specialists something you'd be willing to help manage, or is that typically outside the scope of what you do here?"*

**What you are listening for:** A Coordinating GP will acknowledge the coordination problem as real and common, and will either confirm they actively manage this or be honest about the limits of what they can do. A standard GP will either dismiss the problem or give a vague reassurance without any concrete framing of how they would approach it.

---

## Step 7 — Assess the Full Appointment

After the consultation, before you do anything else, write down your observations while they are fresh. Use this framework:

| Signal | Observed | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Engaged with the SBAR summary specifically | Yes / No / Partial | |
| Asked a clarifying question about your conditions | Yes / No | |
| Mentioned a specialist network or referral relationship | Yes / No | |
| Acknowledged care coordination as a real challenge | Yes / No | |
| Showed curiosity rather than deflection at complexity | Yes / No | |
| Left adequate time for your questions | Yes / No | |
| Reaction band | A / B / C / D | |

---

## Step 8 — The Decision

**Band A:** Book a follow-up. Begin sharing more of the picture over the next 1–2 appointments. This is your GP.

**Band B:** Book one follow-up with a specific clinical question — something from your Open Questions list. See if they engage substantively. Decide after that appointment.

**Band C or D:** Move to the next candidate on your shortlist. Do not invest further time here.

**No candidates passed:** Return to `doctor-sourcing-guide.md` and broaden your search. Consider asking a specialist for a GP recommendation directly.

---

## What Comes Next

Once you have confirmed a Coordinating GP, the relationship deepens gradually:

1. **Appointment 1 (this appointment):** Mindset assessment only. SBAR summary. Vetting questions.
2. **Appointment 2:** Bring one specific clinical question. Assess how they handle it. Begin sharing more context.
3. **Appointment 3 onward:** The full picture, including your index.md and relevant modules, can be introduced. At this point you are working with a confirmed partner.

See `appointment-prep-template.md` for how to prepare each subsequent appointment within the Australian system's time constraints.
