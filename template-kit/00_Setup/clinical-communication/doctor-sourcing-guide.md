# Doctor Sourcing Guide — Finding a GP Who Can Manage Complexity
> **Scope:** Australian healthcare system. Focused on finding a GP suited to complex, multi-system chronic illness — referred to here as a "Coordinating GP."
> **When to use:** When you are looking for a new GP, or evaluating whether your current GP is the right long-term partner for a complex case.
> **Next step after this guide:** Use `vetting-interview-script.md` to confirm the fit before committing.

---

## The Core Problem

The Australian healthcare system is built around high-volume, episodic care. The standard 10-minute consultation model works well for acute illness and routine management. It does not work for patients whose conditions span multiple systems, require longitudinal tracking, and sit at the edges of specialist boundaries.

Finding a GP who is genuinely suited to complex chronic illness requires active sourcing — not just choosing the nearest bulk-billing practice. The difference between a GP who manages complexity well (Coordinating GP) and one who does not (standard GP) is not about their knowledge base alone. It is about their practice style, their curiosity, and the structural conditions of where they work.

---

## Before You Start — Generate Your Personalised Criteria

The collaboration signals in Step 2 apply to any complex patient. But *which* signals matter most for your specific conditions — and which specialist connections would be most valuable in a Coordinating GP — depends on your health picture. Before searching, use your AI assistant to generate a personalised criteria list from your `index.md`.

**Paste the following prompt into your AI assistant, followed by the contents of your `index.md`:**

---

*"I am looking for a Coordinating GP — a GP suited to managing complex, multi-system chronic illness longitudinally. Based on my health wiki index below, please identify:*

*1. Which medical sub-specialty interests would be most relevant for a GP managing my active conditions — list them in priority order*
*2. Which specialist referral relationships would be most valuable for this GP to already have in their network*
*3. Which of my current care coordination gaps (from my Care Team and Open Questions sections) a Coordinating GP would most need to actively bridge*
*4. Any upcoming treatment decisions in my open questions that require specific GP knowledge, comfort with uncertainty, or willingness to coordinate off-label or complex pharmacological discussions*

*Format the output as a prioritised list I can paste directly into a GP candidate evaluation worksheet."*

---

Save the AI's output. You will paste it into **Part A** of the candidate worksheet (`gp-candidate-worksheet.md`) — one copy per candidate you evaluate.

## Step 1 — Where to Look

### Practice websites
Most Australian GP clinics have websites. The quality and depth of those websites is itself a signal. Look for:
- Named GP profiles with more than a single line of text
- Stated areas of clinical interest (not just "all ages, all conditions")
- Any mention of: chronic disease management, complex cases, metabolic health, gastroenterology interest, women's health beyond routine, functional medicine, preventive health with a metabolic focus
- Allied health services co-located at the practice (dietitian, exercise physiologist, psychologist — see Step 2)

### HotDoc and Healthengine
Both platforms list GP practices and sometimes individual GPs with stated interests. Use the search filters. "Chronic disease" and "complex care" are worth filtering for. Read the individual GP profiles where available — they vary significantly in depth.

### Hospital websites and affiliated practices
Some GPs hold conjoint appointments or visiting medical officer (VMO) roles at public or private hospitals — particularly teaching hospitals. These are often listed on hospital department pages or the GP's practice bio. This connection is one of the most reliable positive signals (see Step 2).

### AHPRA registration lookup
The Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency (AHPRA) register at **ahpra.gov.au** lets you verify:
- That a GP is currently registered and in good standing
- Their registration type and any conditions on practice
- Their principal place of practice

AHPRA does not provide clinical detail — use it for verification, not sourcing.

### Word of mouth from specialists
If you already have a specialist (gastroenterologist, gynaecologist, endocrinologist), ask them directly: *"Do you have any GPs you work with regularly who are comfortable with complex, multi-system patients?"* Specialists who work with good GPs know who they are. This is the highest-quality referral source available.

### Rare disease and chronic illness patient communities
Online communities (Facebook groups, forums, Reddit) for specific conditions often contain local knowledge about GPs who are known to take complex cases seriously. This is anecdotal but often more current than any directory.

## Step 2 — What to Look For: The Collaboration Checklist

Use this checklist when evaluating a GP profile or practice — it forms **Part B** of the candidate worksheet (`gp-candidate-worksheet.md`). No single signal is definitive — look for patterns.

### Strong positive signals

- [ ] **Teaching hospital connection** — current or past VMO role, conjoint appointment, or fellowship at a major teaching hospital (e.g. Royal Melbourne, Alfred, Royal Brisbane, Royal Prince Alfred, Westmead, Flinders Medical Centre). Teaching hospital experience correlates strongly with comfort managing non-textbook cases and collaborating with specialists.

- [ ] **Allied health co-location** — the practice employs or shares rooms with a dietitian, exercise physiologist, clinical psychologist, or other allied health. This indicates a practice model oriented toward holistic, chronic disease management rather than transactional throughput.

- [ ] **Chronic Disease Management (CDM) plan experience** — some GPs specifically advertise CDM plans (Medicare items 721/723) or Team Care Arrangements. This is a structural signal: these items require the GP to coordinate care, which is exactly the mindset needed.

- [ ] **Stated sub-specialty interest that overlaps with your conditions** — metabolic health, women's health (beyond routine Pap smears), gastroenterology interest, complex pain, sleep medicine, autoimmune conditions. The more specific, the better.

- [ ] **Longer appointment slots available** — if the practice offers 30- or 40-minute appointments as a standard option, the practice model is designed for depth. Practices that only offer 10- or 15-minute slots structurally cannot support complex case management.

- [ ] **FRACGP + additional training** — Fellowship of the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners is standard. Additional qualifications (DRANZCOG for obstetrics, advanced skills in skin cancer, sports medicine, or emergency medicine) indicate a GP who has invested beyond the minimum — and often indicates curiosity and capacity for complexity.

- [ ] **Works in a mixed-billing or private practice** — not a red flag in itself, but bulk-billing-only practices are under structural pressure to maximise throughput, which compresses consultation time. Mixed-billing or fully private practices typically allow longer appointments and often attract GPs who prefer depth over volume.

### Weak or neutral signals
- Long time at the same practice (can mean stability or can mean stagnation — check against other signals)
- Large practice with many GPs (more likely to have at least one Coordinating GP, but less likely to maintain continuity)
- "Family medicine" or "all ages" as the primary stated focus — not negative, just uninformative

### Red flags
- Practice website lists only: skin checks, workplace medicals, travel vaccinations, and standard immunisations — high-volume transactional model
- No named GP profiles, or profiles with a single sentence only
- "Bulk-billing only, no gap payments" combined with no statement of complex care interest — structural throughput pressure
- Online booking system only allows 5- or 10-minute slots with no option for longer
- GP bio mentions "corporate health" or "occupational health" as primary interest — optimised for a different patient population

## Step 3 — What You Cannot Reliably Find Online

Be realistic about what this process can and cannot surface. Most Australian GP bios are sparse. Do not over-interpret a thin profile as a red flag — some excellent GPs have minimal web presence. The sourcing process narrows the field; the vetting appointment (see `vetting-interview-script.md`) is where you actually confirm the fit.

What you cannot reliably determine from a profile:
- How much time the GP actually spends per patient in practice
- Whether their stated interests translate into clinical depth
- Their actual specialist network and referral relationships
- Their receptivity to a patient who has done significant self-research

What you can determine from a profile:
- The structural model of their practice (appointment lengths, allied health, billing model)
- Stated interests and any verifiable affiliations
- Teaching hospital connections (often verifiable via hospital websites)
- Whether they have invested in sub-specialty training

## Step 4 — Shortlist and Next Steps

Aim for 2–3 candidate GPs from this process. You do not need to find the perfect GP in the sourcing stage — you need a credible shortlist to take into the vetting appointment.

**For each candidate:** Complete a copy of `gp-candidate-worksheet.md` — copy the file, rename it (e.g. `gp-candidate-smith.md`), fill in Parts A and B from your research, and record your shortlist decision. The worksheet produces a ranked shortlist that feeds directly into the vetting appointment.

**Then proceed to `vetting-interview-script.md`** — the first consultation is where you confirm whether the fit is real. Book a long consultation (Level C, 20–40 min) for each shortlisted candidate.

---

## A Note on Your Current GP

If you already have a GP you have been with for some time, this guide is not necessarily a prompt to leave. It is a framework for evaluating whether your current GP is suited to the complexity of your case — and if not, for finding one who is. It is possible to have one GP for routine matters and to seek a second opinion or a new primary GP for complex case management without ending the existing relationship.
